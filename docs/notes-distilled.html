/preface

Guide is intentionally context-free / metagame-agnostic — designed to teach how to think not what to think. Aspires to be a "teambuilding partner or battling coach frozen in time." Three audiences: noobs, intermediates seeking new perspective, experienced players.

/introduction-to-competitive-pokemon

VGC = doubles, 6 Pokémon team, 4 brought into each battle after team preview. "Two Pokémon fight against two Pokémon at a time" — synergy is mandatory, not optional. Strategic, not reflex-driven game.

/coming-from-single-battles (CRITICAL for user)

Unlearn from singles:

  • Counter-based thinking — in doubles, opponent partners can protect/support, so a single counter isn't enough.
  • Setup strategies — opponents can double-target the setup mon or its partner. Setup needs enablers (Follow Me, Fake Out).
  • Hazards (Stealth Rock, Spikes) — "they just don't do very much" with only 4 mons brought in.

Build new habits:

  • Protect on nearly every Pokémon. Lets one mon act freely while partner is safe.
  • Spread move math: AoE moves (Heat Wave, Earthquake, Rock Slide, Dazzling Gleam) deal 75% damage when hitting multiple targets. Heat Wave that OHKOs in singles is a 2HKO in doubles.
  • Double targeting: focus fire to KO threats before they act.
  • Positioning + redirection: Follow Me, Rage Powder, Ally Switch.
  • Teambuilding: pick TWO leads (~half the team), not one. Many teams use "modes" — synergistic pairs (e.g. Drizzle + Swift Swim).
  • Tempo: matches are 10-12 turns / 10-15 min. No stall.

/the-basics-of-watching-a-pokemon-match

For beginners: track health bars, super-effective callouts, KO counts. Don't worry about damage calc or team-preview reasoning yet — that comes later. Recognize luck moments (crits, misses, status, flinch) as part of the game, not as outcomes to dwell on.

/everyone-is-learning-all-the-time

Everyone — including World Champs — has knowledge gaps and forgets things mid-match. Don't self-judge. Learn the top ~50 mons of a format first; that's enough foundation. Knowledge accumulates passively through play; don't try to cram.

/context-pt1

A Pokémon isn't "good" or "bad" — only good relative to the metagame. Build awareness via: (1) tracking what you face on ladder, (2) Pikalytics usage stats, (3) tournament results. Resources are descriptive not prescriptive. Don't chase trends weekly, but keep awareness.

/context-pt2

"Theorymonning" = mentally simulating battle scenarios pre-game. Solo theorymonning has blind spots — collaborative theorycrafting reveals gaps. Play extensively to build intuition. After a tournament/loss: analyze where your prediction diverged from outcome, integrate the lesson. Don't ask "does X beat Y?" — ask "in what context does X beat Y?"

/teambuilding-introduction

Teambuilding = "an art, not a science." 7 axes to balance: synergy, consistency, pacing, breadth, depth, dynamicness (pair-strength), relevance (vs. meta). Beginner sequence: (1) decide whether to build or use someone else's, (2) if building, START with intent — what is this team trying to do? — before anything else.

/how-to-use-someone-elses-team (CRITICAL early-stage path)

"Reverse engineer" a recently-published team with author notes:

  1. List threats you expect to face.
  2. Examine how mons cover each other's weaknesses.
  3. Identify which moves you'll use most & why unusual choices exist.
  4. Identify intended "modes" / pair combinations.
  5. Develop pre-planned team-preview approaches for predictable matchups.

Don't memorize the original pilot's plays — you're "playing their team in your way." Old teams may not match current meta. Don't get lost in EV calc weeds. Celebrate small wins — recognizing "this decision feels right" = real growth.

/speed-control (CRITICAL — user said they don't know this)

Wolfe Glick: attacking first is "an enormous advantage." If you KO before they act, their move never executes. Gen 8+ uses dynamic speed (recalculated mid-turn).

Methods (have ≥2 on every team):

  • Tailwind: 2x speed, 4 turns. Setters: Whimsicott, Talonflame. Loses to Trick Room.
  • Trick Room: reverse speed, 5 turns. -7 priority so can't go first turn 1. Most iconic form, won 7/12 Worlds historically. Setters need Fake Out / Follow Me protection.
  • Speed drops (Icy Wind, Electroweb, Bulldoze): less committal, fits anywhere. Reset on switch. Strong late-game.
  • Speed boosts (Dragon Dance, Quiver Dance): persist through switches. Weather speed (Swift Swim, Sand Rush, Chlorophyll, Slush Rush). Choice Scarf.
  • Priority moves (Grassy Glide, Extreme Speed, Sucker Punch): bypass speed entirely. Rillaboom + Grassy Surge enables priority Glide.
  • Natural speed: forces opponent to invest in their own speed control.
  • Thunder Wave (90% acc, fails vs Electric/Ground), Quash, Trick + Lagging Tail (gimmick).

/protect

"Protect allows you to control the pace of the game." Mind game value > damage prevention. Use to: safely switch, stall out opponent's Tailwind/TR/weather, enable supportive setup behind it.

When to NOT run Protect:

  • Bulky walls with recovery (Cresselia, Dusclops, Porygon2).
  • Choice item users (need flexibility).
  • Specialists with Fake Out / dual screens (better utility).
  • Pure offense, coverage-heavy.

Heuristic: if your mon takes ≥50% from common threats, Protect is essential. Test the team without it on specific mons to feel the gap.

/trick-room

Reverses move order by speed for 5 turns. Slow heavy hitters become S-tier. Team has 3 roles: setter (bulky like Cresselia or offensive like Hatterene), helper (Fake Out / redirection to protect setter), sweeper (slow, bulky, coverage).

Counterplay: Taunt (block setup), Roar/Dragon Tail (force switch before setup), re-Trick-Room to cancel, run your own slow mon to flip the advantage. Hybrid speed teams (3 fast + 3 slow, e.g. Arash Omatti 2013 Worlds) are flexible.

/typing

Type effectiveness is contextual. Don't try to cover all types — focus on what's common in current meta. Bug/Poison weaknesses matter less (those moves are rarely used). Stakataka 2019 example: Steel typing valuable because it resisted common threats, not in isolation. Goal: each mon's typing should let it switch in on attacks that would hurt teammates. Avoid picking mons "just for typing" if they lack stats/utility.

/utility-moves

Categories:

  • Protect — foundational. Almost everyone runs it.
  • Speed control — Tailwind, Trick Room, Icy Wind/Electroweb (hybrid: damage + speed drop).
  • Redirection — Follow Me, Rage Powder. Completely shields partner from single-target attacks.
  • Screens — Reflect/Light Screen/Aurora Veil. Reduce damage to ⅔ for 5–8 turns.
  • Taunt — kills setup mons & recovery walls.
  • Stat drops — Fake Tears, Breaking Swipe (Atk drop on AoE).
  • Anti-setup — Haze (clears all stat changes), Roar/Dragon Tail (force switch). Fail vs Dynamax.
  • Fake Out — 100% flinch, only turn 1 of being out. Tempo king.
  • Setup moves — DD/NP/QD. Need protection (Fake Out partner / redirection).
  • Recovery — works for very bulky mons taking ~40% from neutral hits.
  • Status — ranking: freeze > sleep > burn >> paralysis >>> poison.
  • Weather setting — manual Sunny Day/Rain Dance, override opposing weather.
  • Ally Switch — swap positions, redirect attacks. Generates "difficult decisions for both players."

/items

  • Choice items (Band/Specs/Scarf) — max damage / speed, locked into one move. Use when "best move is clearly best."
  • Life Orb — 1.3x damage, 10% recoil, no lock-in.
  • Type-boosting items (Magnet, Mystic Water, etc.) — gentler damage boost, preserves flexibility.
  • Sitrus Berry — heals at 50% HP. For mid-bulk mons that survive 2–4 hits.
  • Leftovers — passive walls, Leech Seed / terrain stackers.
  • Focus Sash — guaranteed survival from full HP. For frail attackers.
  • Assault Vest — 1.5x SpDef, no status moves. For mixed-bulk attackers.
  • Type-resist berries (Shuca, Coba, etc.) — 50% resist a single super-effective hit.
  • Safety Goggles — immunity to powder moves & weather damage.
  • Mental Herb — first instance of Taunt/Encore/Attract is shrugged off.
  • Light Clay — extends screens to 8 turns.

Selection rule: same item can "bolster a strength" or "mitigate a weakness." Estimate how often it activates; weigh vs. opportunity cost.

/synergy

Offensive synergy — partners cover each other's offensive blind spots:

  • Different damage profiles (one threatens A, other threatens B → opp can't switch into either).
  • Cross-coverage: e.g. Groudon KOs the Steel mons that block Xerneas; Xerneas handles Ground/Water that beat Groudon.
  • Damage stacking: Helping Hand + boosts → "types almost don't matter."
  • Setup-only enabling ≠ synergy. Both must contribute KOs.

Defensive synergy = "switchability." Two mons synergize if they can switch in on threats to each other. FWG (Fire/Water/Grass) is the classic example. Primarina/Ferrothorn — Primarina eats Fire/Fighting aimed at Ferro, Ferro eats Grass/Electric aimed at Primarina.

Anti-synergy: e.g. Tapu Fini's Misty Surge blocks Amoonguss' Spore. Watch for these conflicts.

Synergy needs vary by meta. Don't formulate; study top teams to see format-specific patterns.

/cores-and-modes

Core = a group whose synergy defines the team. Build remaining slots to support the core. Mode = a pair/group with a specific game plan, may be lead-together or situational.

  • Teambuilding modes: intentional pairings (e.g. Snorlax + Mimikyu Trick Room + Z-Destiny Bond, Nils Dunlop 2018).
  • Battling modes: emergent pairings discovered through practice that you reuse as a game plan.

Famous cores:

  • FWG (Fire/Water/Grass) — classic. AFK (Arcanine/Tapu Fini/Kartana) dominated VGC17.
  • Dragon/Fairy/Steel ("fantasy") — VGC14 Aegislash/Hydreigon/Mega-Mawile.

Modes can restrict team-preview options or telegraph plans — use carefully.

/archetypes

Archetype = "a core with defined strengths and weaknesses that really defines a team." Emerges when many similar teams converge on a pattern (e.g. "Standard Zacian" = Zacian + FWG of Incineroar/Urshifu-R/Rillaboom).

Archetypes evolve with meta. Don't memorize a static list — check Victory Road tournament results to see what's currently winning. When using an archetype, learn its known weaknesses and try to solve them rather than blindly copy.

/metagame

Wolfe's methods: (1) play and note what you see often, (2) Pikalytics for usage stats (approximate, "doesn't tell the full story"), (3) watch streamers/YouTubers at your rating tier, (4) compare notes with friends. "Keeping up with the metagame can be like drinking from a firehose" — general awareness is enough; you don't need to be 100% current.

/6th-pokemon-syndrome

Trap: you have 5 mons and keep cycling the 6th searching for a magic fix. The real problem is the 5-mon core has structural weaknesses no single mon can fix.

Solutions: (1) test more options before assuming the slot is the issue — if you're scraping low-usage picks, you're reaching; (2) accept "every team has weaknesses, and yours is not an exception"; (3) if truly stuck, scale back to 2–3 essentials and rebuild, or scrap entirely.

/how-to-beat-a-pokemon

3-step framework:

  1. Identify strengths — its offensive coverage, defensive utility (Fake Out, Intimidate, sleep), speed advantages, why it frustrates you specifically.
  2. Exploit weaknesses — super-effective coverage, speed control to outpace, disrupt its weather/terrain dependency, Taunt to neutralize utility, etc.
  3. Test rigorously — confirm theoretical advantages survive practical games.

For combos (not single mons): "synergy against synergy." Identify the enemy synergy, then build your synergy that breaks it.

Caveats: no perfect KO % in doubles (partner support skews things). Some mons are too central to counter with a single mon — need the team to have the answer. Don't tunnel-vision into hyper-specialized counters that lose elsewhere.

/competitive-pokemon-is-less-luck-than-you-think

Aaron Zheng: top players "consistently maneuver themselves to maximize their own luck." Compare to poker. Don't blame losses on luck — that mindset blocks improvement. Things you control:

  • Move accuracy: don't run sub-90% moves casually. (His 2014 nationals: Stone Edge cost games.)
  • Speed control: moving first dodges crit/effect variance.
  • Defensive durability vs setup: bulky games extend battle, raise crit count. Hyper-offense reduces variance windows.
  • Status prep: every team needs status answers (immunity / cleric / cure berry).
  • Tilt management: practice mental reset between games; don't dwell on hypotheticals.

/building-up-a-knowledge-base

Aaron Zheng's 3-tier knowledge stack — focus on top ~20 mons in current format:

  1. Move pools: top 6 moves per mon. Categorize: >25% usage (high), <25% (moderate), rare. Since mons only have 4 slots, uncommon moves come at cost of common ones — exploitable.
  2. Items: cover everything ≥10% usage. Items often "dictate how the Pokémon is used" (Choice Scarf vs AV vs Sash = different role).
  3. Training (EV spreads): recognize common speed benchmarks, offense vs bulk patterns. Don't memorize spreads — recognize directions.

Tool: Pikalytics. Goal: "identify every single option your opponent has" before they reveal it.

/team-preview (CRITICAL — 90s decision)

Step 1 — Read opponent:

  • Spot obvious combos (ability synergies, Fake Out + attacker, speed control, redirection, Weakness Policy setups).
  • Assess opp speed dynamics overall.
  • Identify any "1-vs-4 threats" (mons that can solo your team if uncontested).

Step 2 — Eliminate backwards: pick the 2 of your 6 to leave behind based on:

  • weak offensive typing into their team,
  • weak defensive typing into their team,
  • redundant role,
  • weak when separated from a partner,
  • bad speed dynamic.

Step 3 — Confirm essentials: lock in restricted legendaries / primary attackers; verify you can deal meaningful damage to every one of their mons (especially threats with sweep potential).

Step 4 — Lead choice:

  • Anticipate their likely lead pair.
  • Can you deploy your obvious combo turn 1, or do they directly counter?
  • Each lead must serve a purpose: speed control set, offensive pressure, defensive stability.
  • Don't lead your "ace" carelessly — losing it early is catastrophic.
  • Don't lead 2 supports — too defensively passive.
  • Bench must enable your lead's plan to work.

Bo3 mode: game 1 = experiment / gather info. After each game: was lead matchup good? Which opp mons hurt you? Could a benched mon have helped? Track revealed movesets — by game 3 you should have near-full info.

Narrative approach: before turn 1, write the story in your head — pressure points, expected progression, your win condition, their win condition.

/what-is-a-game-plan

"A game plan is a general approach to how you plan on winning a battle." Components:

  • Team mastery: know your spreads/move choices well enough that exceptions are conscious. Example: knowing your specific Speed-invested Incineroar + Tailwind OHKOs Zacian-C with Flare Blitz.
  • Opp analysis: how can their team neutralize your strengths? Find typing gaps, speed-tier reliance, early-vs-late game dependencies.
  • Win condition: what's your specific path to victory this game? Eliminate their X? Position your Y? Stall their Dynamax?
  • Pace: does your lead apply pressure? What happens when your speed control wears off? Can you close out without "running out of steam"?
  • Turn-1 rehearsal: pre-game, simulate turn 1 against likely lead pairs. Identify a "safe" play that wins / draws against most opp choices.

Game plans are adaptive — they're a starting frame, not a script.

/what-is-pressure

"The threat of proactive action" = pressure. Pikachu pressures Gyarados because Pikachu can KO it.

Sources: KO threat, speed advantage, status threat (Sleep Powder), setup threat (forced response).

Reading pressure = reading the opp's mind. Ask each turn: who moves first? what KOs are on the board? if both sides hit their main goal, who wins this turn? Knowing what they fear tells you what they'll do.

Apply pressure to dictate their move set — narrows their options, makes them predictable.

/predictions

Aaron Zheng's 2-part frame:

  1. Anticipate every combination of opp moves → find the play that handles them all.
  2. Anticipate a specific opp play → react accordingly (read).

When to predict:

  • Ahead: only if right = win and wrong ≠ immediate loss.
  • Neutral: needs significant upside.
  • Behind: predict when no safe play covers all options, or when "safe" still loses.
  • Bo1: predict less — surprise weighs more.

How to predict well: meta knowledge (common items/moves/spreads) + battle-specific tells (revealed info, opp behavior).

Don't fetishize successful reads. Building a team that requires fewer reads is often better than getting good at reading.

/protect-in-battle

When: KO threats, status threats, repositioning, stalling out opp's Tailwind/TR/Dynamax.

Before pressing Protect, ask:

  • Is my mon actually in danger this turn?
  • Does my partner address the threat regardless?
  • What does next turn look like after Protect succeeds? Have I changed anything?

Common mistake — "Protect Extravaganza": Protect repeatedly without changing the board. Solves nothing.

Reading opp Protect: usage varies wildly by mon. Series 12 example — only 5% of Incineroar ran Protect, but 97% of Zacian did. Pikalytics shows you who to attack vs who to play around.

"Forcing" them to Protect: if you attack when they expect to be in danger, they Protect. Now you've burned their Protect turn and the next turn opens up. Consecutive Protect = ⅓ success — do not double-Protect except as last resort.

/switching

When to switch:

  • Survival (KO threat).
  • Positioning for next turn's expected state.
  • Field condition (TR/Tailwind expiring) — bring in the mon that uses the remaining window best.
  • Resource preservation — keep the closer healthy for endgame.

Costs:

  • Incoming mon eats a hit unprotected — risk crit/burn/freeze.
  • Switch ≠ damage. Doesn't progress toward their KO count.
  • Switch fails if your switch-in is also bad into the matchup.

Switching is a prediction. Honestly imagine the opp's worst-case response. Will they actually do it? If yes, you may not want to switch.

/how-to-analyze-a-battle (CRITICAL POST-GAME LOOP)

After every game (especially losses), review in this order:

  1. Pivotal turn — the one that decided the match.
  2. Suboptimal turns — where play or prediction failed.
  3. Mon selection — should you have brought the bench mon?
  4. Lead matchup — was it actually winnable?
  5. Overall matchup — was the team itself losing this fight regardless?

For each turn ask:

  • Did I make the mathematically best play (cover-all-options)?
  • Did I correctly read their specific move?
  • Would my play change if I'd known their move?
  • Did my play take unnecessary risk even though it worked?

RNG is the LAST excuse, not the first. "In Pokémon, luck is a skill — bad luck usually means you made a teambuilding or playing mistake."

Avoid results-based analysis: a play that worked isn't automatically right. Evaluate decision quality independent of outcome.

Red flags suggesting bad team, not bad play: constantly needing risky reads to win, no mon answers a specific opp threat, your nukes don't OHKO, no neutral lead option, "I played well and still lost."

/1-hp-is-infinitely-more-than-0-hp

A mon at 1 HP does the same damage as a healthy one. Don't auto-write off low-HP mons. Decision frame:

  1. Can I safely bring it back later (Volt Switch, after a KO, prediction switch)?
  2. Will it impact future turns (priority move, ability, threat)?

Even as pure sacrifice fodder it's useful — re-trigger Intimidate next switch-in, eat a hit so partner can act, stall opp Trick Room.

Switching costs are real — weigh against gains. But "1 HP > 0 HP" is the heuristic to fight your singles instinct.

/what-is-a-good-ladder-rating (CRITICAL — directly answers user's goal)

Aaron Traylor's Showdown ELO brackets:

  • 1000–1200: new. Quirky teams, players from other formats. Builds adaptability fundamentals.
  • 1200–1400: moderately competitive — climbed past default. More informed predictions.
  • 1400–1600: "good battles that make me think seriously." Tournament-quality practice. This is the user's target ceiling.
  • Top 500+: requires consistently high WR. Diminishing returns above this.
  • Top 100 / 10: tiny error margins.
  • Rank 1: minimal points per win, big losses per loss — patience over aggression.

Battle Stadium: Master Ball tier = solid first goal. Top 30 by month-end = ambitious.

Caveats: ladder ≠ tournament skill. Bo1 ladder tests different skills than Bo3. High-ladder play gets insular (familiar opponents = prep biases). Don't make ladder rating identity. Use it as a rough benchmark, not self-worth.

/intent

"Intent — or the reasoning behind your decisions and choices — is the backbone of all things teambuilding." Every choice should have a why.

Valid starting intents:

  • Interest-driven: "I want to make Groudon work."
  • Meta-driven: "These two threats keep killing me, I need a counter."
  • Strategic positioning: "I need a sweeper enabler / defensive pivot / status absorber."

Why beginners need this: when something underperforms, intent lets you diagnose — was it bad design, or bad play? Without intent, you can't tell.

Gut-instinct picks aren't wrong, but you should be able to articulate the gut feel.

/consistency

A consistent team wins the average game, not just the optimal game. Principles:

  • Run utility moves that work regardless of opp choice: Fake Out, Taunt, Haze, Safeguard. Avoid niche reactive moves like Lucky Chant or "Roar that backfires."
  • Strategy works whether opp knows your team or not. Surprise ≠ strength.
  • Test on ladder, in mock Bo3 vs unfamiliar opps, vs prepped opps, vs counter-prepped opps.
  • Avoid relying on sub-100% accuracy moves casually. Sleep Powder only when missing isn't costly.
  • Build navigations toward endgame states where you're at ~100% to win.

/analyzing-your-opponents-teams (8-category checklist)

  1. Offensive threats — primary damage dealers, setup users, dynamax candidates.
  2. Speed control — Tailwind, TR, Icy Wind/Electroweb, T-wave, Scary Face. How they pair it (e.g. Tailwind enables partner's nuke).
  3. Pace — fast/slow team. Early dynamax or stall?
  4. Speed benchmarks — does your key threat naturally outspeed theirs?
  5. Type coverage — favorable/unfavorable matchups.
  6. Pairings — synergistic duos likely to lead.
  7. Support:offense ratio — exploit any imbalance.
  8. Item distribution — predict items, especially when multiple mons want the same one.

Goal isn't perfect prediction — it's pattern recognition. Train this and you can read 6 mons → core game plan in seconds.

/battling-against-trick-room

Two opening questions: (1) Can I stop TR from setting up? (2) If it does, how bad is that for me?

Tactics:

  • Time-waste: TR is 5 turns, effective 4. Double Protect kills 2/4. Add defensive switches, Fake Out lockdowns, status to stall further.
  • Positioning: don't auto-KO their support (e.g. Indeedee-F) turn 1. Leaving it forces them into bad decisions — keep it (vulnerable to your defense) or switch out (exposing the sweeper). Reduces their effective TR window from 4 to 2.
  • After TR expires: identify exactly what's needed to prevent re-setup, plan which mons close out post-TR.
  • Hyper-offense struggles vs TR (no defensive tools). Balance has more answers.

/approaching-best-of-1-vs-best-of-3 (CRITICAL — user is on ladder = Bo1)

Bo1 ladder mindset:

  • Surprise wins games. Unconventional set on conventional mon, or unpopular mon with little prep.
  • Tempo > endurance. Fast offense wins more games per unit time. Slow teams expose more secondary effects.
  • Risk-averse leads — avoid lead matchups that lose immediately.
  • Conservative play — assume opp has their strongest moves.

Bo3 tournament mindset (user not pursuing, but for context):

  • Consistency > surprise. Multiple modes, multiple lead options.
  • Information-gathering across games.
  • More calculated risk-taking based on revealed info.
  • Adapting between games is THE skill.

/you-dont-need-to-teambuild-to-play-pokemon (CRITICAL early-stage)

"You can't play competitive Pokémon without battling, but you can play without building." Beginners trying to build AND learn battling at once = compounded difficulty.

Use established teams from tournament players or community resources. When you lose, it's easier to diagnose: "was that play wrong, or was the team wrong?" Borrowed teams isolate the variable that matters most early — your battling skill.

If you genuinely prefer battling over building, that's a valid permanent path. Many top players use teams others built.

/your-team-determines-your-luck

Luck isn't random — your team choices determine your exposure. Concrete:

  • Accuracy: run 100% acc moves where possible. If you must run an inaccurate move, give the mon a backup reliable option.
  • Speed: moving first dodges crit/secondary effect/flinch variance.
  • Composition balance: hyper-frail teams die to single misfortunes (domino effect). Hyper-defensive teams take so long they invite crits/effects (e.g. Calm Mind stacks → vulnerable to crit through boosts).
  • Status: Tapu Fini's Misty Surge etc. eliminates an entire variance category.

"Lucky" players engineer luck through teambuilding. Not innate.

/what-makes-a-pokemon-good

"A definition you have to come to yourself" — but the dimensions are:

  • Field presence: forces opp to respond. Can't be safely ignored.
  • Consistency: works even when opp knows the set. Doesn't require setup or low-acc to function.
  • Stat alignment: stats fit the role (not all max — but max in what matters).
  • Matchup coverage: neutral-to-favorable vs common meta threats without needing weather/TR.
  • Team synergy: enables/complements teammates. Even attackers can support by drawing focus.
  • Strategic flexibility: multiple ways to win with the same mon.

High usage ≠ good. (Pachirisu won Worlds 2014.) Articulate why a mon meets your criteria before adding it.

/how-do-you-know-if-a-team-youve-made-is-good (4 indicators)

  1. Fun — engaging? losing still leaves you wanting another game?
  2. Control — proactive: opp reacts to YOUR threats. "Pokémon should do the work" — winning shouldn't require constant heroic play.
  3. Weakness profile — every team has weaknesses; the question is whether they map to common meta or rare picks. Distinguish "hard but winnable with skill" from "nearly unwinnable regardless."
  4. Consistency — works when opp knows your strategy.

Iterate if: fun + proactive + manageable weaknesses + consistent. Scrap if: not fun + reactive + unavoidable hard matchups vs common threats + inconsistent across practice sets.
