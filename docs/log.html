🔒 Your log lives only in your browser

This site has no server. Everything you record is stored in your browser's localStorage and never leaves your device. Other visitors to this site have their own separate logs in their own browsers — your data is invisible to them.

If you clear your browser data, switch to incognito mode, or use a different device, your log doesn't follow you automatically. Download a backup at least weekly using the button on the dashboard.

📋 The 6 threat-type axes — reference card
This is the checklist behind the "Threat-type checklist done pre-T1?" tag in the game logger. At team preview, scan the opponent's 6 against each of the 6 axes below. Most preview-stage losses come from missing ONE of these axes — the Orthworm Body Press trap, the Cloud Nine surprise, the Psychic Terrain Quash-blocker. You don't have to verbalize all 6 every game; just sweep through them so none gets missed.

1. ⚔️ Immediate damage

The axis: Can the opponent KO your support or anchor right now (turn 1–2)?

How to spot it: spread attackers in their leads (Astral Barrage / Dazzling Gleam / Heat Wave types), Choice-locked nukes, super-effective coverage against your defensive mons.

Why it matters: if your support dies T1, every plan downstream fails. Address this first in your scan.

Counter pattern: screens / Wide Guard / Protect the fragile mon / lead defensively if the threat is severe.

2. 📈 Setup scaling

The axis: Does anything on their team become unbeatable if ignored for 2+ turns?

How to spot it: Iron Defense + Body Press, Calm Mind / Dragon Dance / Quiver Dance / Bulk Up users, weather or terrain abusers that snowball.

Why it matters: easy to under-rate at preview because the mon looks passive. By turn 4 it's the actual win condition. This is the trap that cost Game 6 in your last 10.

Counter pattern: pressure setup mons early, even if they look harmless. Two free turns is the deciding gift.

3. 🌀 Position disruption

The axis: Can they break your formation — force switches, redirection-breakers, Ally Switch?

How to spot it: Roar / Whirlwind / Dragon Tail in their pool, Intimidate-cycling, Ally Switch users, taunt + phazing combos.

Why it matters: your team relies on specific pairs staying together (e.g. Sableye + Archaludon as Mode A). Phazing invalidates the pair-mode; you have to re-establish.

Counter pattern: have a defensive structure that survives one phazing turn. Don't put both your win-cons next to each other if they show Roar.

4. ☁️ Weather / terrain denial

The axis: Can they nullify your weather or field condition?

How to spot it: Cloud Nine / Air Lock abilities, opposing weather setters that overwrite yours, terrain setters that block your priority or status.

Why it matters: your Electro Shot tempo depends on rain. A Cloud Nine mon sitting there means your damage axis breaks. This is the Drampa lesson from Game 10.

Counter pattern: bring a backup setter (Pelipper), or plan for unstable weather — don't click moves that require rain.

5. ⚡ Priority denial

The axis: Can they stop your priority-control plays — Quash, Fake Out, Prankster moves?

How to spot it: Psychic Terrain (blocks priority moves on grounded mons), Queenly Majesty / Dazzling / Armor Tail / Mind's Eye abilities, Misty Surge (blocks status priority).

Why it matters: Sableye's Quash + Fake Out is half your speed control. Lose it and the team's tempo axis collapses.

Counter pattern: identify the priority-denier at preview. Remove it before pressing priority-dependent moves.

6. 🛡️ Endgame wall

The axis: Can ONE of their mons solo-beat your remaining team late-game?

How to spot it: bulky walls with recovery (Cresselia / Snorlax archetypes), setup sweepers that snowball past a single check, mons your team has no super-effective answer to.

Why it matters: even if you control turns 1–8, you can't close. The classic "I was winning but couldn't finish" loss.

Counter pattern: plan how Basculegion or Archaludon removes this specific mon before endgame. If you can't, mark the matchup as "play around" — preserve a closer, don't trade evenly.

How it's used at team preview: in your 90-second window, sweep their 6 against each axis. You'll usually only flag 1–3 axes per game — most opps have one or two specific threats. Once you flag them, your Turn-1 plan must address each. Missing an axis is how Phase 1 losses happened (the Orthworm, the Drampa, the Farigiraf).
📖 How Phase 2 works — quick reference

🔧 One-time setup (~90 min)

  1. Speed map — Team tab → ⚡ Speed map. ~30 min.
  2. Defensive benchmarks — Team tab → 🛡️ Benchmarks. ~45 min.
  3. Paste the AI's support-mon decision table into your team's reverse-engineering notes (Team tab).
  4. Start the reading list — knock out 1–2 of the 6 articles here.

🎮 Per ladder session

  1. 5-min pre-session damage calc on Pikalytics — top 3 likely opp attackers vs your team.
  2. Play 2–3 games, stop after 3 consecutive losses.
  3. Per game: log + fill Phase 2 tags.
  4. Per loss: 5-min replay review using the 5-question framework.

🧠 During every game (in-game habits)

  1. Pre-Turn-1 threat-type checklist — 6 axes.
  2. Support-mon first-move audit.
  3. No autopilot Electro Shot (or your team's equivalent default move).
  4. Single-sentence commitment: "The Pokémon I must not allow free turns is ___."

📅 Weekly

  1. 1 replay drill — pause a pro replay before each of 3 turns, predict, then watch.
  2. 1–2 more readings until all 6 done.
  3. 1 prediction drill when you do a hard-test game (5 sessions total across the phase).

🔁 Every ~10 games

  1. Dashboard → Backup & export → 📋 AI summary (.md)
  2. Drop into ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini, ask for Mode A review.
  3. Run Mode B on the most informative loss.
  4. Paste the AI's upgraded lesson back into that game's lesson field.

✅ End of Phase 2 (only when ready)

  1. Self-attest the 6 checkpoint items below. Evidence required to pass.
  2. Run Mode C on the AI — Phase 2 readiness audit.
  3. If 4+ checkpoints honestly pass, advance to Phase 3.
